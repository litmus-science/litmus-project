## Why I Became an Operator

I'm in year 4 of my PhD. My stipend is $32,000/year—not enough for the Bay Area. I was looking for side income that actually used my skills.

Traditional options sucked:
- Tutoring: $30/hour but scheduling headaches
- Consulting: Requires reputation I don't have yet
- Food delivery: Doesn't use my training

Litmus let me monetize skills I already have and equipment I already access. I run experiments for others during gaps in my own research.

## Getting Verified

The verification process took about a week:

1. **Identity verification**: Standard ID check
2. **Institutional affiliation**: I uploaded my student ID and a letter confirming my lab membership
3. **PI approval**: My advisor signed a form confirming I could do external work using lab resources during approved hours (evenings and weekends). Our university has a policy for this—it's not unusual.
4. **Competency assessment**: I claimed proficiency in bacterial culture, MIC assays, antibiotic susceptibility testing, basic molecular biology. They asked me to describe my experience and provide references to papers I'd contributed to.

Once approved, I could see jobs matching my verified skills.

## What Makes a Good Job

I look for:

**✅ Good jobs**:
- Clear protocols (templates are best)
- Reasonable timelines (>1 week)
- Materials provided or standard reagents
- Specific acceptance criteria
- Fair budget for the work

**❌ Jobs I skip**:
- Vague protocols ("test if this works")
- Rushed timelines (<3 days)
- Weird requests that seem off
- Budgets way below market rate
- Anything outside my competency

## Quality and Documentation

Litmus requires documentation I'd do anyway for good science:

- **Photos of key steps**: I photograph plate setups, results, anything that shows the work was done properly
- **Raw data files**: Plate reader outputs, images
- **Lab notebook entries**: I keep notes anyway; scanning the relevant pages takes 2 minutes

This protects me too. If there's a dispute, I have evidence the work was done correctly.

## Handling Problems

**Contamination**: Happened once. I documented it, reported immediately, and re-ran the experiment. The platform extended the deadline automatically. No penalty because I was transparent.

**Unexpected results**: A compound was supposed to be active based on the requester's prediction. It wasn't. I documented everything, triple-checked my positive control (which worked), and submitted the negative result. The requester approved it—they wanted the truth, not confirmation of their hypothesis.

**Requester dispute**: One requester claimed my results were wrong because they didn't match their expectations. Platform arbitration reviewed my documentation, confirmed the protocol was followed correctly, and ruled in my favor. Payment released.

## Tips for New Operators

1. **Start with templates you know**: Don't claim custom protocols until you're comfortable with the platform.

2. **Communicate early**: If something's unclear, ask before starting. Requesters appreciate it.

3. **Document everything**: Photos take 30 seconds and save hours of dispute resolution.

4. **Be honest about timelines**: If you can't start for a week, say so. Requesters prefer honesty over optimistic promises.

5. **Quality over quantity**: Your reputation score matters. One bad job can tank your match rate.

## The Bigger Picture

I'm contributing to science I couldn't do alone. Some of these experiments are genuinely interesting—people testing traditional remedies, AI companies validating predictions, hobbyists with creative hypotheses.

The money helps, but there's also something satisfying about helping democratize lab access. Not everyone has a PhD program to get them into a BSL-2 facility. Litmus lets curious people participate in real science.

When I graduate, I might keep doing this part-time. The income is decent, the work is flexible, and I get to see a wider slice of science than my narrow dissertation topic.
